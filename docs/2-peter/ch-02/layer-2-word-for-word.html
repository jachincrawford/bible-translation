KEY TERMS — v.1: - pseudodidaskaloi [NT-HAPAX] — ψευδοδιδάσκαλοι: "false teachers." Only occurrence in NT. Compound of pseudēs (false) + didaskalos (teacher). Cf. pseudoprophetai (false prophets) in same verse — deliberate parallelism. - pareisaxousin — παρεισάξουσιν: from παρεισάγω (pareisago). "Bring in alongside/secretly." Prefix para + eis + ago. The "smuggling" nuance of covert introduction is load-bearing. - haireseis — αἱρέσεις: from αἵρεσις. "Heresies" here means destructive factions/sects; the word originally meant "choice" or "school of thought." In context it is modified by apoleias (of destruction) — "heresies of destruction." - apoleias — ἀπωλείας (occurrence 1 of 3 in ch. 2): gen-sg. Track: v.1 (haireseis apoleias), v.1 again (tachinon apoleian), v.3 (he apoleia auton). A dominant motif. - agorasanta — ἀγοράσαντα: from ἀγοράζω. Redemption/purchase term. The Master "bought" (purchased/redeemed) these false teachers — they deny the one who owns them. Strong market/ransom resonance. - despoten — δεσπότης: "Master/Owner/Sovereign." Not the common kyrios (Lord) but the absolute-ownership term. Intensifies the scandal of the denial. - tachinon — ταχινήν: "swift/imminent." Same word used in 1:14 of Peter's own imminent death (NOTE: lexical echo with 1:14 — tachinē there of Peter's departure; here of the false teachers' destruction). - apoleian (occurrence 2 of 3 in ch. 2) — verse-final, emphatic position.

KEY TERMS — v.3: - pleonexia — πλεονεξίᾳ: "greed/avarice/covetousness." The root motive: having-more (pleon + echō). Financial exploitation of community members is the charge. - plastois — πλαστοῖς [NT-HAPAX]: from πλαστός. "Fabricated/molded/made-up." Only NT occurrence. Used of pottery/sculpted objects — the words are artfully manufactured fictions. Devastating rhetorical image. - emporeusontai — ἐμπορεύσονται: from ἐμπορεύομαι. "Will traffic in/exploit as merchandise." Commercial term — treating persons as commodity for profit. - krima — κρίμα: "judgment." The legal verdict (already standing) — not a future decision but a present juridical reality. - ekpalai — ἔκπαλαι: "from long ago/since of old." [Rare term] — Appears only here and in 3:5 in NT. The antiquity of the condemnation underscores its certainty. - nystazei — νυστάζει: from νυστάζω: "slumbers/nods off." Vivid anthropomorphic metaphor — their destruction is not asleep/lethargic. The same verb is used of the foolish bridesmaids in Matt 25:5 (only two NT occurrences). - apoleia (occurrence 3 of 3 in ch. 2): now as subject — "their destruction does not slumber." The motif completes here.

VARIANT: σειροῖς (seirois, "pits/dungeons," N1904 text) vs. σειραῖς (seírais, "chains," attested in several important MSS including Codex Alexandrinus and P72). The N1904 follows σειροῖς (pits of darkness); most modern critical editions (NA28/UBS5) follow σειραῖς (chains). The two readings produce distinct images: imprisonment in pits vs. binding with chains. See Section 1 Textual Notes.

KEY TERMS — v.4: - tartarosas — ταρταρώσας [NT-HAPAX]: from ταρταρόω. "Having cast into Tartarus." Only NT occurrence. Tartarus is the Greek mythological underworld prison for the Titans — here appropriated for the realm of imprisoned angels. Peter's use of Greco-Roman cosmological vocabulary is culturally significant. - seirois/seirais — σειροῖς/σειραῖς [VARIANT noted above]: "pits" or "chains." The ambiguity is not merely textual but theological — confinement vs. binding. - zophou — ζόφου: from ζόφος. "Darkness/gloom." Not the common skotos but the Homeric/poetic term for pitch darkness. Appears again in v.17 (zophos tou skotous — "gloom of darkness"). - tereo — τηρέω (teroumenous): "being-kept/reserved." The divine custody motif. The keeping is ongoing (present participle) — they are currently held for future judgment. Verb echoes 1:19 and recurs in v.9 and v.17.

KEY TERMS — v.5: - ogdoon — ὄγδοον: "eighth/as-one-of-eight." Noah as the eighth person preserved (eight souls in the ark: 1 Pet 3:20). The numbering emphasizes the minority preserved amid universal judgment. - dikaiosynes keruka — δικαιοσύνης κήρυκα: "herald of righteousness." kerux (κῆρυξ) is the herald/town-crier who proclaims officially. Not simply righteous himself but active proclaimer. A rare and significant designation for Noah, found outside canonical OT (cf. Jewish tradition). - kataklysmon — κατακλυσμόν: from κατακλυσμός. "Flood/inundation." The same cataclysmic event alluded to in 3:6. This verse and v.6 together form the two exempla of divine judgment: Flood and Sodom/Gomorrah. - asebon — ἀσεβῶν: from ἀσεβής. "Ungodly-ones." Genitive plural modifying kosmo. The "world of the ungodly" — the flood brought upon an ungodly world (kosmo asebon = genitive of characterization).

KEY TERMS — v.6: - tephrostas — τεφρώσας [NT-HAPAX]: from τεφρόω. "Having reduced to ashes." Only NT occurrence. The catastrophic finality is encoded in the term itself — not merely burned but turned to ash/cinders. Strong volcanic imagery (cf. the region's geology). - katastrophe — καταστροφῇ: "overthrow/catastrophe." Dative of means or manner: "condemned with/by overthrow." The English "catastrophe" is a direct loan from this Greek word. - hupodeigma — ὑπόδειγμα: "example/specimen/pattern." The judgment of Sodom/Gomorrah is set forth as a model — a standing exhibit of what awaits the ungodly. The word is carefully positioned: the verdict is didactic, not merely punitive. - asebein — ἀσεβεῖν: pres-act-inf from ἀσεβέω. "To be ungodly/to act impiously." The infinitive after mellonton: "for those who are about to be ungodly" — the judgment is proleptic and universally applicable.

The verb of divine rescue — God as Deliverer. Also in v.9 (rhyesthai). Forms the theological spine of the triad: judgment (vv.4–6) paired with rescue (vv.5, 7).

KEY TERMS — v.8: - blemati — βλέμματι [NT-HAPAX]: from βλέμμα. "Sight/gaze/seeing." Only NT occurrence. The noun form of blepō. Lot's very act of seeing was a form of torment — the sight itself was painful. - enkatoikon — ἐγκατοικῶν [NT-HAPAX]: from ἐγκατοικέω. "Dwelling-among/residing-within." Only NT occurrence. The compound intensifies the sense of being embedded in a hostile environment. - hemeran ex hemeras — ἡμέραν ἐξ ἡμέρας: "day from day / day after day." Temporal idiom expressing unrelenting duration — the torment was continuous. - anomois ergois — ἀνόμοις ἔργοις: "lawless works." Dative plural. The instrument of torment — his righteous soul tormented by their lawless acts. The righteous soul in the midst of the lawless community. - ebasanizen — ἐβασάνιζεν: impf-act from βασανίζω. "Was being tortured/tormented." Imperfect = ongoing/repeated action. Originally used of testing metals on a touchstone; then of torture; here of inner anguish.

KEY TERMS — v.11: - ischyi kai dynamei meizonas — ἰσχύϊ καὶ δυνάμει μείζονες: "greater in strength and power." The angels' superiority to the false teachers is underscored — and yet even they do not bring a blasphemous charge. The argument is a fortiori: if the mighty restrain themselves, how much more should these presumptuous men. - pherousin — φέρουσιν: from φέρω. "Bring/bear." NOTE: φέρω echo from ch. 1 — the same verb used of the prophetic word "borne along" by the Holy Spirit (1:21, pheromenoi). Here φέρω appears in the judicial sense: the angels do not "bring" a blasphemous judgment/charge. The verbal echo links prophetic inspiration (ch.1) with judicial restraint (ch.2). - blasphemon krisin — βλάσφημον κρίσιν: "blasphemous judgment/charge." The very thing the false teachers do without restraint (v.10), angels refuse to do even against those they could legitimately condemn. Striking reversal.

They blaspheme in the things they are ignorant of — ironic and damning.

KEY TERMS — v.22: - alethous paroimias — ἀληθοῦς παροιμίας: "the true proverb." The proverb is introduced as genuinely true (alethous — "true/real"). Two proverbs are cited — the first from Prov 26:11 (dog returning to vomit), the second from an unidentified source (the washed pig). - Kyon — κύων: "dog." In ancient Jewish thought, a highly pejorative term. The dog "returns to its own vomit" (Prov 26:11 LXX: kuon epistrepsas epi ton heautou emetou) — Peter adapts/echoes the LXX closely. - exerama — ἐξέραμα: "vomit." From exerao. A vivid and deliberately coarse term — the thing expelled from the body returned to. Cf. Prov 26:11 (LXX emetos = vomit). The exerama here (only NT occurrence of this specific form) emphasizes the repulsive nature of the return. - Hys — ὗς: "sow/pig." Female swine. The second proverb. "A sow having washed, to wallowing in mud." The washing (bathing) was the moment of cleansing — the baptism echo is widely noted in the scholarly tradition. The sow returns to what is natural to her. - borborou — βορβόρου: "of mud/filth/mire." Gen-sg from βόρβορος. The destination of the washed pig — the mire from which she came. The final word of the chapter. Thematically, it answers the opening — those brought in by false teachers, those who seemed to escape (v.20), those who knew the way of righteousness (v.21) — they return to the βόρβορος. The chapter ends in the mire. - NOTE: The double proverb (dog + pig) forms a deliberate rhetorical climax: two animals, two returns to their respective pollution — complete and unreserved apostasy. Cf. the closing structure of the Sermon (Matt 7) with its contrasting pairs.
