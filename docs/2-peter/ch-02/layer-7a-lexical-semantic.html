Overview: The Vocabulary of 2 Peter 2

Chapter 2 is the most lexically aggressive passage in 2 Peter. It concentrates nine NT hapax legomena within twenty-two verses, sustains four interlocking term clusters across the full chapter, and closes with a citation from Proverbs that imports OT proverbial vocabulary into the letter's rhetorical climax. The chapter's vocabulary organizes into five semantic fields: destruction, moral excess, judicial custody, commercial exploitation, and speech and its corruption — all bearing on the characterization of the false teachers.

Cluster 1: ἀπώλεια — The Destruction Motif

The noun ἀπώλεια ("destruction/ruin/perdition") appears three times in the chapter and functions as a framing device. At [v.1] it appears twice in rapid succession: first as the genitive modifier of αἱρέσεις — "heresies of destruction" (αἱρέσεις ἀπωλείας) — and immediately again as the object of ἐπάγω: these teachers bring "swift destruction" (ταχινὴν ἀπώλειαν) upon themselves. The doubling within a single verse is not accidental; it embeds the verdict in the very introduction of the subject. By [v.3], ἀπώλεια reappears as the subject of a clause — "their destruction does not slumber" (ἡ ἀπώλεια αὐτῶν οὐ νυστάζει) — now personified and active, paired with the anthropomorphic image of a wakeful, un-drowsing sentence. The lexical range of ἀπώλεια in the NT runs from simple loss (Matt 26:8) through eschatological doom (Rev 17:8, 11); here the eschatological register is operative. The term tracks the false teachers from introduction to verdict.

Cluster 2: ἀσέλγεια — Moral Excess as Signature Term

The noun ἀσέλγεια, denoting wanton, unrestrained licentiousness or sexual excess, anchors the ethical profile of the false teachers across three points in the chapter. At [v.2] it appears in the plural (ταῖς ἀσελγείαις) — "their licentious behaviors" — as the thing many will follow. At [v.7] it recurs in the singular, coloring the description of Sodom: Lot is rescued from men living "in the licentiousness of conduct" (ἐν ἀσελγείᾳ ἀναστροφῆς). The linkage is deliberate — the same term attaches to the ancient Sodomites and to the present false teachers, identifying the latter as typological recurrences. At [v.18] it appears again in the plural, paired with ἐπιθυμίαις σαρκός: the teachers entice through "fleshly lusts, licentious behaviors." The distribution across vv.2, 7, and 18 brackets the entire chapter, marking ἀσέλγεια as a signature term for the opposition.

Cluster 3: The βλασφημέω Motif

The blasphemy vocabulary saturates the central portion of the chapter, appearing at [v.2] (βλασφημηθήσεται), [v.10] (βλασφημοῦντες), [v.11] (βλάσφημον κρίσιν), and [v.12] (βλασφημοῦντες) — four occurrences in eleven verses. The semantic range of βλασφημέω covers both defamatory speech against persons and impious speech directed at divine beings or sacred realities. At [v.2], the object is "the way of truth" (ἡ ὁδὸς τῆς ἀληθείας) — an institutional or communal entity brought into disrepute. At [v.10–12], the object shifts to δόξας (discussed below) — celestial beings or dignities the teachers revile without restraint. The irony at [v.11] is lexically acute: angels, who are μείζονες ἰσχύϊ καὶ δυνάμει ("greater in strength and power"), do not bring a βλάσφημον κρίσιν even against those they could legitimately condemn. The men who blaspheme most freely are precisely those least qualified to do so — the a fortiori argument is embedded in the term's distribution.

Cluster 4: τηρέω — The Judicial Thread

The verb τηρέω ("to keep, guard, reserve") weaves a judicial thread through the judgment exempla (vv.4–17). At [v.4], the fallen angels are "being kept" (τηρουμένους, present passive participle) in pits of darkness for judgment — the custody is ongoing. At [v.9], the unrighteous are kept (τηρεῖν) "while being punished" (κολαζομένους) for the day of judgment — the punishment is not merely anticipated but concurrent with the holding. At [v.17], the reserved gloom of darkness (τετήρηται, perfect passive) awaits the false teachers — the perfect tense encoding a completed, irrevocable reservation. The progression from present participle [v.4] through present infinitive [v.9] to perfect indicative [v.17] tracks an intensifying certainty: the custody is active, ongoing, and already accomplished. τηρέω in this chain is not a word of protection but of judicial confinement.

Hapax Inventory: Nine Unique Lexemes

Chapter 2 concentrates nine NT hapax legomena, each precisely positioned.

ψευδοδιδάσκαλοι [v.1] — the compound false-teacher noun, appearing nowhere else in the NT. Its construction deliberately mirrors ψευδοπροφῆται in the same verse, yoking the new phenomenon (false teaching in the church) to the well-mapped category of false prophecy in Israel. The semantic pairing transfers the whole OT judgment apparatus onto the contemporary situation.

πλαστός [v.3] — "fabricated/molded," from the root of pottery and sculpture. Applied to λόγοι (words), it characterizes the teachers' speech as artfully manufactured fictions — objects shaped to purpose, not organically true. The manufacturing metaphor is devastating precisely because it is calm and technical.

ταρταρόω [v.4] — a denominative verb coined from Tartarus, the Greek mythological sub-Hades prison for the Titans. Its use imports Greco-Roman cosmological vocabulary into a Jewish-Christian judicial framework. The word is neither LXX nor common Koine — it signals deliberate engagement with the Hellenistic cosmological imagination of the audience.

τεφρόω [v.6] — "to reduce to ashes," from τέφρα (ash/cinder). The finality encoded in the term — not merely burning but complete incineration — is matched by its positional function: Sodom and Gomorrah are the second exemplum of judgment, and the term ensures the image is maximally complete.

βλέμμα [v.8] — the noun form of βλέπω, "sight/gaze." Lot's very act of seeing was torment; the noun isolates the visual perception itself as the locus of suffering. Paired with ἀκοή ("hearing"), it creates a sensory diptych: sight and sound were both instruments of ongoing distress.

ἐγκατοικέω [v.8] — "to dwell-among/reside-within." The compound intensifies the sense of embeddedness — Lot was not a visitor but a permanent resident in hostile territory. The spatial depth of the compound (κατ- + οἰκ-) encodes entrapment.

τολμητής [v.10] — "daring-one/presumptuous-one." The noun designates reckless boldness in venturing where one has no right to go. Its pairing with αὐθάδεις ("self-willed/headstrong") constructs a portrait of two reinforcing character flaws: presumptuous action and inflexible self-regard.

ἀκατάπαυστος [v.14] — "unable to cease/insatiable." Applied to ὀφθαλμούς ("eyes full of an adulteress and of sin"), it encodes an appetite that resists all arrest — the sinful gaze that cannot be stopped. The alpha-privative compound (a- + katapauō, "to cause to cease") packages the idea of pathological incapacity.

παραφρονία [v.16] — "madness/insanity," from παρά (beside, off) + φρήν (mind/reason). The noun characterizes Balaam's behavior as outright irrationality — and the vehicle of his rebuke is a voiceless animal (ἄφωνον), which speaks while the prophet himself operates in madness. The irony is lexically embedded: ἄφωνον (without voice) and παραφρονίαν (without reason) are the two privative characterizations of Balaam's encounter — the animal possesses the reason the prophet has lost.

δεσπότης vs. κύριος: Ownership and Lordship

The choice of δεσπότης at [v.1] rather than the far more common κύριος is semantically significant. κύριος (occurring at [v.9] and throughout 2 Peter) denotes lordship in the sense of authority, honor, and the relational title "Lord." δεσπότης denotes absolute ownership — the master who holds complete legal title over a slave or household. The false teachers deny the one who "bought" them (ἀγοράσαντα, aor. ptcp. from ἀγοράζω — market/purchase language). The compound image is of the owner who paid the purchase price now being denied by those he owns. The switch to δεσπότης is not interchangeable with κύριος; it selects the property-ownership register of the slave market over the honor-authority register of lordship. The scandal of the denial is proportional to the completeness of the ownership asserted.

ἐμπορεύομαι: Commercial Exploitation Language

At [v.3], the verb ἐμπορεύομαι ("to traffic in, exploit as merchandise") is used of the false teachers' treatment of the community: "in greed, with fabricated words, they will exploit you" (ὑμᾶς ἐμπορεύσονται). The word's root is ἔμπορος — the merchant, the trader, the one who deals in commodities. Applied to persons, it converts people into inventory. The commercial register is precise: the false teachers are not merely manipulative; they are operating a commercial model in which the community members are the product being traded on. Combined with πλεονεξίᾳ ("greed/avarice") and πλαστοῖς λόγοις ("fabricated words"), the verse assembles a portrait of organized commercial fraud, with the vocabulary drawn entirely from trade and manufacture.

δόξας: Semantic Range and Interpretive Crux

At [v.10], the false teachers are said to blaspheme δόξας ("glories/dignities"). The term is the crux of the verse's interpretation. The semantic range of δόξα in Greek extends from "opinion/reputation" through "honor/renown" to its NT-dominant sense of "glory/radiance" — and in certain plural usages, to "angelic beings/celestial powers/dignities." The parallel passage in Jude 8 (δόξας βλασφημοῦσιν) offers no disambiguation but confirms the motif. In context — sandwiched between the refusal of angels to bring blasphemous charges [v.11] and the irrationality of blaspheming what one does not know [v.12] — δόξας most plausibly denotes celestial authority structures: angelic beings or classes of heavenly powers. The false teachers revile what they do not understand; the word δόξας holds the semantic weight of dignities that command awe.

φθορά: Wordplay at v.12

Verse 12 deploys φθορά in a sustained paronomasia across three morphological forms. The false teachers are compared to irrational animals born εἰς ἅλωσιν καὶ φθοράν ("for capture and destruction," φθοράν accusative). They blaspheme in what they are ignorant of, and ἐν τῇ φθορᾷ αὐτῶν ("in/by their corruption," φθορᾷ dative) they will also φθαρήσονται ("be destroyed/corrupted," future passive from φθείρω). The same root — φθορά/φθείρω — appears as the end-purpose of their existence (accusative), the character of their condition (dative), and the verb of their fate (future passive). The triple repetition is a deliberate rhetorical figure: born for destruction, operating in destruction, destined to be destroyed. The word's semantic range covers both moral corruption and physical annihilation — the wordplay keeps both registers simultaneously active.

ἐλευθερία → δεδούλωται: The Freedom/Slavery Irony

At [v.19], the chapter's sharpest semantic reversal is achieved through direct antithesis: ἐλευθερίαν αὐτοῖς ἐπαγγελλόμενοι, αὐτοὶ δοῦλοι ὑπάρχοντες τῆς φθορᾶς — "promising freedom to them, [while] they themselves are slaves of corruption." The noun ἐλευθερία ("freedom/liberty") is the advertised product; the noun δοῦλοι ("slaves") is the actual status of the sellers. The emphatic pronoun αὐτοί underscores the contrast: the very ones promising freedom are themselves in the most complete bondage. The following epigram — "by whatever a person has been overcome (ἥττηται), to this he has been enslaved (δεδούλωται)" — deploys two perfect passives whose aspect encodes settled, accomplished states. The irony is not merely rhetorical but lexical: ἐλευθερία belongs to the vocabulary of release and legal freedom; δεδούλωται belongs to the vocabulary of permanent enslavement. The distance between the two terms is the measure of the deception.

v.22: Prov 26:11 — Lexical Echo

The chapter closes with a citation of Proverbs 26:11 LXX: κύων ἐπιστρέψας ἐπὶ τὸ ἴδιον ἐξέραμα ("a dog having returned to its own vomit"). Peter's version at [v.22] maintains the key lexical items of the LXX: κύων (dog), ἐπιστρέψας (having returned, from ἐπιστρέφω), and the vomit — though substituting ἐξέραμα (from ἐξεράω) for the LXX's ἔμετος. Both terms denote vomit; the substitution involves no semantic shift, but ἐξέραμα is the more forcefully expressive form. The LXX verb ἐπιστρέψας is the same root as ὑποστρέψαι at [v.21] ("to turn back from the holy commandment") — the lexical echo is structural: the false teachers turn back (ὑποστρέφω) from the way of righteousness as the dog turns back (ἐπιστρέφω) to its vomit. Two compounds of the same root (ὑπο- and ἐπι-) map human apostasy onto the proverb's animal behavior. The second proverb — the washed sow returning to the mud (βόρβορος) — has no canonical LXX source; it closes the chapter on the final word of the chapter, βορβόρου, placing the muddy destination as the chapter's last lexeme and the image of contaminated return as its last meaning.
