KEY TERMS — v.1: - diegeiro — διεγείρω: "stir up/arouse/awaken." Same verb used in 1:13 (diegeirein) — Peter's repeated purpose statement. He is "awakening" their mind by means of reminder. The continuity with the opening chapter is deliberate. - eilikrine — εἰλικρινῆ: from εἰλικρινής. "Sincere/pure/unmixed." Etymology debated: either "judged by sunlight" (heilē + krinō) or "sifted until pure" (heilē + krinō in a different sense). The idea is an uncontaminated, clear-thinking mind — the opposite of the muddled thinking the false teachers produce. - dianoian — διάνοιαν: from διάνοια. "Mind/understanding/thinking faculty." Not merely intellectual but the whole disposition of thought. Peter wants to stir up their "sincere mind" — the cognitive and moral faculty together. - hupomnései — ὑπομνήσει: from ὑπόμνησις. "Reminder/remembrance." The instrument of the stirring. Cf. 1:12-15 where Peter's entire ministry is cast as a ministry of reminder (hupomnēsis). The letter itself is the reminder.

KEY TERMS — v.2: - proeiremenon — προειρημένων: perf-pass-ptcp from προερέω. "Previously spoken/foretold." The words were spoken before and remain in force — the perfect tense emphasizes the continuing validity. Cf. Jude 17 (close parallel). - rematon — ῥημάτων: from ῥῆμα. "Words/utterances." Specifically spoken words or declarations, as distinct from logos (which can be more abstract). The prophets' specific spoken declarations. - entoles — ἐντολῆς: from ἐντολή. "Commandment." Singular — "the commandment of the Lord and Savior through your apostles." The chain of authority: Lord/Savior → apostles → community. A single unified command. - Soteros — Σωτῆρος: from σωτήρ. "Savior." The Granville Sharp construction (tou Kyriou kai Soteros) identifies the Lord and the Savior as the same person. Christological title recurring in 2 Peter (1:1, 1:11, 2:20, 3:2, 3:18).

KEY TERMS — v.3: - empaigmone — ἐμπαιγμονῇ [NT-HAPAX]: from ἐμπαιγμονή. "Mockery/scoffing." Only occurrence in the entire NT. The dative is instrumental — "with mockery." The mockers come armed with ridicule as their characteristic mode. Related to empaizō (to mock/ridicule), used of the mockery of Christ (Matt 27:29, 31). The hapax emphasizes the distinctive quality of this end-times scoffing. - empaiktai — ἐμπαῖκται: from ἐμπαίκτης. "Mockers/scoffers." Rare word — occurs only here and Jude 18 in NT. The agent-noun from empaizō. These are professional scoffers, not casual doubters. The doubling (empaigmonē + empaiktai) — "in mockery, mockers" — is a cognate-accusative-like intensification: they are what they do. - eschaton ton hemeron — ἐσχάτων τῶν ἡμερῶν: "last of the days" — an eschatological formula. Cf. Acts 2:17 (ep' eschatōn tōn hēmerōn), Heb 1:2, James 5:3. Peter places the readers in the eschatological timeframe — the mockers' arrival is itself a sign. - epithymias — ἐπιθυμίας: from ἐπιθυμία. "Desires/lusts." The mockers' motivation: they walk "according to their own desires." The same charge leveled against the false teachers in ch. 2. Their theological skepticism is morally motivated.

KEY TERMS — v.4: - epangelia — ἐπαγγελία: from ἐπαγγελία. "Promise." The specific promise of Christ's parousia — his return. The scoffers target the promise itself as fraudulent. This term recurs in v.9 (tes epangelias) and is answered by the noun epangelma in v.13. - parousias — παρουσίας: from παρουσία. "Coming/arrival/presence." Technical term for Christ's second advent. The genitive is objective — "the promise concerning his coming." The scoffers are parousia-deniers. - ekoimethésan — ἐκοιμήθησαν: from κοιμάω. "Fell asleep" — a euphemism for death. Aorist passive. "The fathers" are likely the first generation of Christians (or possibly OT patriarchs). Their death without the parousia occurring is the mockers' evidence. - diamenei — διαμένει: from διαμένω. "Continues/remains throughout." The prefix dia- intensifies: "keeps on remaining" — unbroken continuity from creation. This is the uniformitarian argument: nature has always been stable, therefore nothing catastrophic will happen. Peter will demolish this premise in vv.5-7. - ktiseos — κτίσεως: from κτίσις. "Creation." The starting point of the mockers' claim — from creation's beginning, everything continues unchanged. Peter will counter: creation itself was not unchanged (the flood).

KEY TERMS — v.5: - lanthanei — λανθάνει: from λανθάνω. "It escapes notice/is hidden from." The construction is significant: "this escapes them willingly" — the ignorance is deliberate. They choose not to know. The verb takes an accusative of the person from whom something is hidden. Cf. v.8 (me lanthaneto humas — "let this not escape you" — the positive counterpart to the mockers' willful ignorance). - thelontas — θέλοντας: from θέλω. "Wanting/being willing." The participle is concessive or causal — "because they want [it to]" or "while willfully [choosing this ignorance]." Their blindness is not innocent — it is chosen. The moral dimension of epistemology. - ekpalai — ἔκπαλαι: "from long ago." Cf. 2:3 where the same rare adverb is used of the false teachers' judgment ("from long ago is not idle"). Peter turns the mockers' own appeal to antiquity against them. - sunestosa — συνεστῶσα: perf-act-ptcp from συνίστημι. "Having been constituted/holding together/consisting." The earth was constituted "out of water and through water" by God's word. The perfect tense: formed in the past with continuing results. The very element (water) that constituted the world was also the instrument of its judgment (v.6). - logo — λόγῳ: from λόγος. "Word." Dative of instrument/means — "by the word of God." The creative word that made the heavens and earth is the same authoritative word that will judge. The parallel with v.7 (to auto logo — "by the same word") is the structural hinge of Peter's argument.

KEY TERMS — v.6: - kataklystheis — κατακλυσθείς: aor-pass-ptcp from κατακλύζω. "Having been deluged/flooded." Only NT occurrence of this verb (the noun kataklusmos appears in Matt 24:38-39, Luke 17:27, 2 Pet 2:5). The passive voice: the world was flooded by divine agency. The same element (water) that constituted the earth (v.5) destroyed it. Peter's counter-argument to uniformitarianism: the world has already been catastrophically judged once. - apoleto — ἀπώλετο: 2aor-mid from ἀπόλλυμι. "Perished/was destroyed." The "then-world" was annihilated. Strong term — total destruction of the antediluvian order. The verb connects to the apoleia (destruction) motif throughout 2 Peter (2:1, 2:3, 3:7, 3:16). - di' hon — δι' ὧν: "through which" — the antecedent is debated: "through which [waters]" or "through which [word and waters together]." The ambiguity may be deliberate — both the word and the water were instrumental.

KEY TERMS — v.7: - tethesaurismenoi — τεθησαυρισμένοι: perf-pass-ptcp from θησαυρίζω. "Having been stored up/treasured." A striking metaphor: the heavens and earth are "treasured up" — stored as if in a treasury — for fire. The verb normally implies storing something valuable; the irony is that they are stored for destruction. Cf. Rom 2:5 (thesaurizeis seauto orgen — "you treasure up wrath for yourself"). - pyri — πυρί: from πῦρ. "Fire." Dative of destination or instrument. The parallel with v.5-6 is exact: old world constituted by water, judged by water; present world stored up by word, reserved for fire. Water → fire is the eschatological progression. - teroumenoi — τηρούμενοι: pres-pass-ptcp from τηρέω. "Being kept/reserved." Present tense — ongoing divine custody. The same verb used in 2:4 (kept for judgment), 2:9 (kept for the day of judgment), 2:17 (gloom of darkness reserved). The tereo motif is a major structural thread in 2 Peter. - apoleias — ἀπωλείας: from ἀπώλεια. "Destruction." Genitive of purpose or content. The day brings both judgment (krisis) and destruction (apoleia) for the ungodly. Cf. 2:1, 2:3, 3:16 — the apoleia thread reaches its cosmic conclusion here. - asebon anthropon — ἀσεβῶν ἀνθρώπων: "ungodly men." The judgment is not indiscriminate — it targets the ungodly. Cf. the aseb- root throughout 2 Peter (2:5, 2:6, 3:7).

KEY TERMS — v.8: - lanthaneto — λανθανέτω: pres-act-imptv from λανθάνω. "Let it not escape your notice." The imperative directly counters v.5 (lanthanei autous — "it escapes their notice"). The mockers willfully ignore; the beloved must not let this escape them. The chiastic structure: v.5 (lanthanei — they ignore) → v.8 (me lanthaneto — do not ignore). - mia hemera / chilia ete — μία ἡμέρα / χίλια ἔτη: "one day / a thousand years." Allusion to Ps 90:4 (89:4 LXX). The divine perspective on time annuls the mockers' argument from delay. What seems like delay to humans is not delay to God. The chiastic reversal (one day = thousand years AND thousand years = one day) shows divine transcendence over temporal categories in both directions. - para Kyrio — παρὰ Κυρίῳ: "with the Lord / in the Lord's estimation." The dative with para indicates perspective — "from the Lord's viewpoint." Human time-measurement is irrelevant to the divine timetable.

KEY TERMS — v.9: - bradynei — βραδύνει: from βραδύνω. "Is slow/delays." Only two NT occurrences (here and 1 Tim 3:15). The Lord is not "slow" regarding his promise — the perceived delay is not delay at all but purposeful patience. - bradyteta — βραδυτῆτα: from βραδυτής. "Slowness/tardiness." Only NT occurrence. The abstract noun for the quality of being slow. What "some" call slowness, Peter reinterprets as patience. - makrothymei — μακροθυμεῖ: from μακροθυμέω. "Is patient/long-suffering." The reinterpretation of "delay": it is not slowness but makrothymia — patience, long-temperedness. God's patience has a redemptive purpose. Cf. v.15 (ten makrothymian soterias — "the patience of our Lord count as salvation"). - boulomenos — βουλόμενος: from βούλομαι. "Wishing/willing/desiring." Stronger than thelō in some contexts — a deliberate resolve. God's positive will: not-wishing any to perish, but-all to reach repentance. One of the most theologically significant statements in 2 Peter. - apolesthai — ἀπολέσθαι: 2aor-mid-inf from ἀπόλλυμι. "To perish." The verb connects to the entire apoleia/apollymi thread — God does not wish the destruction he has nevertheless decreed for the ungodly (v.7). - metanoian — μετάνοιαν: from μετάνοια. "Repentance." The goal of divine patience. Meta + noeo — a change of mind/direction. The delay is for the sake of repentance. - choresai — χωρῆσαι: from χωρέω. "To come/advance/make room." Used here in the sense of "come to" or "advance toward" repentance. The spatial metaphor: there is room for all to move toward repentance.

KEY TERMS — v.10: - rhoizedon — ῥοιζηδόν [NT-HAPAX]: "with a rushing/roaring/hissing sound." Only occurrence in entire NT. An onomatopoeic adverb — the word itself sounds like the cosmic conflagration it describes. Used in classical Greek for the whizzing of arrows, rushing of wind, or crackling of flames. The heavens will pass away with an audible, terrifying noise. - stoicheia — στοιχεῖα: from στοιχεῖον. "Elements/elemental substances." Semantic range is heavily debated: (a) basic physical elements (earth, water, air, fire); (b) celestial bodies (sun, moon, stars); (c) elemental spiritual powers (as in Gal 4:3, 9; Col 2:8, 20). In this apocalyptic context, physical/cosmic elements is most likely — the fundamental building blocks of the material world will be destroyed. - kausoumena — καυσούμενα: pres-pass-ptcp from καυσόω. "Being burned with intense heat." Rare verb — only here and v.12 in NT. The participle describes the means of dissolution — the elements are dissolved by being subjected to intense burning. The fire motif from v.7 reaches its fulfillment. - lythesetai — λυθήσεται: fut-pass from λύω. "Will be dissolved/loosed/destroyed." The basic meaning of lyō is "to loose/untie" — the cosmic structures will be unbound, taken apart. The verb recurs in vv.11 and 12 (lyomenōn, lythesontai) — dissolution is the governing metaphor for eschatological judgment. - heurethesetai — εὑρεθήσεται [VARIANT]: fut-pass from εὑρίσκω. "Will be found/discovered." The N1904 reading without the negative — "will be found" — may mean "will be found [by God for judgment]" or "will be laid bare/exposed." The NA28 reading with ouch ("will not be found") means total annihilation. The variant is one of the most contested in NT textual criticism. - kleptes — κλέπτης: "thief." The day of the Lord comes "as a thief" — unexpected, sudden. Cf. Matt 24:43; 1 Thess 5:2, 4; Rev 3:3; 16:15. A widespread early Christian motif.

KEY TERMS — v.11: - lyomenon — λυομένων: pres-pass-ptcp from λύω. "Being dissolved." Present tense — the dissolution is viewed as already in process, or as an inevitable certainty described as ongoing. Genitive absolute construction: "these things all being dissolved..." - potapous — ποταπούς: from ποταπός. "What sort of / what manner of." An exclamatory interrogative — "what sort of persons must you be!" The question is rhetorical — the answer is implied in what follows. - anastrophais — ἀναστροφαῖς: from ἀναστροφή. "Conducts/behaviors." Plural — "holy conducts." The plural is unusual and may emphasize the manifold expressions of holy living. Cf. 2:7 (the anastrophē of the lawless in Sodom) — the same word used for opposite moral directions. - eusebeiais — εὐσεβείαις: from εὐσέβεια. "Godlinesses/acts of piety." Also plural — "godlinesses." The plural forms (anastrophais, eusebeiais) suggest multiple concrete expressions of holiness and devotion, not abstract qualities. Cf. 1:3, 6, 7 where eusebeia is singular — here the plural multiplies the demand.

KEY TERMS — v.12: - speudontas — σπεύδοντας: from σπεύδω. "Hastening/eagerly desiring." The startling idea that believers can "hasten" the day of God. The verb may mean (a) eagerly desiring (intransitive) or (b) actively hastening (transitive — through holy living, evangelism, prayer). If transitive, Peter teaches that human conduct can accelerate the eschatological timetable. A remarkable theological claim. - parousian tes tou Theou hemeras — "the coming of the day of God." Note: v.10 had "day of the Lord" (Kyriou); here it is "day of God" (Theou). The shift may be stylistic or may distinguish: the parousia of Christ (Kyriou) and the ultimate day of God (Theou) — the final cosmic transformation. - pyroumenoi — πυρούμενοι: pres-pass-ptcp from πυρόω. "Being set on fire." Distinct from kausoō (v.10, 12b) — pyroō emphasizes the ignition, kausoō the intensity of the burning. - teketai — τήκεται: from τήκω. "Is melted/dissolved by heat." The verb is used of metals melting, wax dissolving, ice thawing. Only NT occurrence. A more vivid image than lyō (dissolve/loose) — the elements literally melt. Cf. Isa 34:4 LXX; Mic 1:4 (the mountains melt). The progression: the heavens are dissolved (lythesontai), the elements melt (teketai). - stoicheia (second occurrence) — repeated from v.10, reinforcing the cosmic scope of the dissolution.

KEY TERMS — v.13: - kainous ... kainen — καινούς ... καινήν: from καινός. "New" (qualitatively new, not merely recent). Both heavens and earth are described as kainos — renewed in quality and character. Cf. Isa 65:17, 66:22; Rev 21:1. The eschatological vision is not mere destruction but re-creation. - epangelma — ἐπάγγελμα: from ἐπάγγελμα. "Promise." Rare noun — only here and 1:4 in NT. Distinct from epangelia (v.4, 9) — epangelma emphasizes the content/substance of the promise, while epangelia emphasizes the act of promising. The promise has concrete content: new heavens and new earth. - prosdokomen — προσδοκῶμεν: from προσδοκάω. "We await/expect." First person plural — Peter includes himself among the expectant community. Cf. v.12 (prosdokontas), v.14 (prosdokontes) — the verb occurs three times in three consecutive verses, creating a drumbeat of expectation. - dikaiosyne katoikei — δικαιοσύνη κατοικεῖ: "righteousness dwells/is at home." The verb katoikeō means "to settle down, take up permanent residence" (not paroikeō, "to sojourn temporarily"). Righteousness will not be a visitor but a permanent resident in the new creation. The present tense is futuristic/prophetic — describing the certain future as if present.

KEY TERMS — v.14: - spoudasate — σπουδάσατε: aor-act-imptv from σπουδάζω. "Make every effort/be diligent." Aorist imperative — urgent, decisive action. The same verb used in 1:10 (spoudasate bebaian humon ten klesin poieisthai — "be diligent to make your calling sure") and 1:15 (spoudasō — "I will make every effort"). A signature verb of 2 Peter — effort and diligence are demanded, not passivity. - aspiloi — ἄσπιλοι: from ἄσπιλος. "Spotless/unstained." Cf. 1 Tim 6:14; James 1:27; 1 Pet 1:19 (amōmos kai aspilos — of Christ). The false teachers are described as spiloi kai mōmoi ("spots and blemishes") in 2:13 — the believers are to be the exact opposite: aspiloi kai amōmētoi. The deliberate antithesis is devastating. - amometoi — ἀμώμητοι: from ἀμώμητος. "Blameless/without reproach." Rare word. In contrast to the mōmoi (blemishes) of 2:13. The believer's ethical character is the opposite of the false teacher's moral deficiency. - heurethenai — εὑρεθῆναι: aor-pass-inf from εὑρίσκω. "To be found." Echoes v.10 (heurethesetai — "will be found"). The earth and its works "will be found" (exposed to judgment); the believers are to "be found" spotless. The same verb, two different objects — judgment for the world, vindication for the faithful. - eirene — εἰρήνῃ: from εἰρήνη. "Peace." In contrast to the cosmic upheaval just described. The believers are to be found in peace — the eschatological shalom. Cf. the letter's opening (1:2, eirene plethyntheie — "peace be multiplied").

KEY TERMS — v.15: - makrothymian — μακροθυμίαν: from μακροθυμία. "Patience/long-suffering." Picking up from v.9 (makrothymei). The Lord's patience is to be interpreted as salvation — not as delay but as redemptive opportunity. The accusative is the object of hegeisthe (consider). - soterian — σωτηρίαν: from σωτηρία. "Salvation." Predicate accusative — "count the patience... as salvation." The equation: patience = salvation. The delay is itself salvific. - Paulos — Παῦλος: "Paul." The only explicit reference to Paul by name in the Catholic Epistles. Peter calls Paul "our beloved brother" — a remarkable statement of apostolic collegiality. Peter claims Paul as an ally who taught the same things. - dotheisan ... sophian — δοθεῖσαν ... σοφίαν: "the given-to-him wisdom." Paul's wisdom is not self-generated but divinely given (dotheisan — aor-pass, divine passive). Peter acknowledges Paul's inspired wisdom while also noting it can be misused (v.16). - egrapsen — ἔγραψεν: aor-act from γράφω. "Wrote." Refers to Paul's letters — Peter assumes his audience knows Paul's correspondence. The reference is general, not to a specific letter.

KEY TERMS — v.16: - dysnoeta — δυσνόητα [NT-HAPAX]: from δυσνόητος. "Hard to understand." Only NT occurrence. Compound: dys- (difficulty prefix) + noeo (to perceive/understand). Peter concedes that Paul's writings contain material that is difficult — an honest acknowledgment. The difficulty is in Paul's letters, not in Paul's intent. - amatheis — ἀμαθεῖς [NT-HAPAX]: from ἀμαθής. "Unlearned/ignorant/untaught." Only NT occurrence. Alpha-privative + manthano (to learn). Those who have not learned — not merely uneducated but willfully unlearned, refusing instruction. The pairing with asteriktoi (unstable) suggests moral as well as intellectual deficiency. - asteriktoi — ἀστήρικτοι: from ἀστήρικτος. "Unstable/unsettled." Cf. 2:14 (psychas asteriktous — "unstable souls"). The same word used of those whom the false teachers entice. The unstable are vulnerable to distortion — they twist Paul's writings as they twist "the rest of the scriptures." - streblosousin — στρεβλώσουσιν [NT-HAPAX]: from στρεβλόω. "Will twist/distort/wrench." Only NT occurrence. The verb is used of torture by twisting on the rack (stroblē = torture instrument). They do violence to the text — stretching and wrenching it beyond its meaning. A remarkably vivid image of hermeneutical abuse. - graphas — γραφάς: from γραφή. "Scriptures/writings." The critical phrase: they twist Paul's letters "as also the rest of the scriptures (tas loipas graphas)." This implies Paul's letters are being placed in the same category as "scriptures" — one of the earliest attestations of Pauline epistles being regarded as Scripture. - apoleian — ἀπώλειαν: from ἀπώλεια. "Destruction." Final occurrence of the apoleia motif in 2 Peter. The chain: 2:1 (×2), 2:3, 3:7, 3:16. Those who twist Scripture do so "to their own destruction" — hermeneutical violence is self-destructive.

KEY TERMS — v.17: - proginoskontes — προγινώσκοντες: from προγινώσκω. "Knowing beforehand." Present participle — "since you know in advance." Their foreknowledge (through this letter and apostolic teaching) gives them responsibility. They cannot claim the willful ignorance of the mockers (v.5). - phylassesthe — φυλάσσεσθε: pres-mid-imptv from φυλάσσω. "Guard yourselves." Middle voice — reflexive: "keep watch over yourselves." Cf. 2:7 (ton athesmon en aselgeia anastrophes — "the lawless ones' licentious conduct" — of those in Sodom). The same word links the eschatological deceivers to the paradigmatic wicked of the OT. - synapachthentes — συναπαχθέντες: aor-pass-ptcp from συναπάγω. "Having been carried away together." Compound: syn + apo + ago — "led away together with." The image is of being swept along by a current — the error of the lawless has a carrying force. Related to asteriktoi (unstable, v.16) — the unstable have no sterigmos; the believers must guard theirs.

KEY TERMS — v.18: - auxanete — αὐξάνετε: pres-act-imptv from αὐξάνω. "Grow!/Increase!" The final imperative of the letter — and it is a command to grow. Present tense: keep on growing, continuously. The letter that began with the divine power granting everything needed for life and godliness (1:3) ends with a command to grow in the grace and knowledge of the One who grants it. - chariti — χάριτι: from χάρις. "Grace." The sphere of growth. Grace is not static — one grows in it. The word occurs only here in 2 Peter (remarkable for a letter about false teaching) — the letter's final word-before-doxology is grace. - gnosei — γνώσει: from γνῶσις. "Knowledge." The second sphere of growth. Cf. 1:2, 3, 5, 6, 8 and the cognate epignosis (1:2, 3, 8; 2:20). Knowledge is a dominant theme of 2 Peter — the letter ends as it began, with knowledge of Christ. Not the false gnosis of the teachers but genuine, growing knowledge. - Kyriou ... kai Soteros Iesou Christou — the full Christological title. Granville Sharp construction: "our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ." Cf. 1:1, 1:11, 2:20, 3:2. The letter's final Christological affirmation matches its first. - hemeran aionos — ἡμέραν αἰῶνος: "day of eternity." An unusual and striking phrase — not "forever and ever" (eis tous aionas ton aionon) but "unto the day of the age/eternity." The singular "day" of eternity contrasts with the temporal "days" that will pass away. The eschatological "day" that swallows all other days — the day that has no evening. - amen — ἀμήν: Hebrew loanword confirming the doxology. The letter's final word. The community's affirmation of the glory ascribed to Christ.
