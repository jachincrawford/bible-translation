2 Peter 1 — L7C Discourse Analysis

2 Peter, Chapter 1

Macro-Structure: 2 Peter as a Whole

Section Content Function
1:1–2 Salutation Establishes authority and leveling
1:3–21 Theological Foundation "You have everything you need — now build"
2:1–22 Warning Against False Teachers The threat to the foundation
3:1–18 Eschatological Certainty The final vindication of the foundation

Chapter 1 is the theological foundation: it establishes what God has done (vv.3–4), what the believer must do (vv.5–11), and why the apostolic/prophetic testimony is trustworthy (vv.12–21). Everything in chapters 2–3 presupposes this foundation.

Chapter 1 — Discourse Map

UNIT 1: Salutation (vv.1–2)

Function: Establishes authority, establishes equality, signals grace-and-peace orientation

Connective structure: - Sender → recipients → benediction (standard Hellenistic letter opening) - The ἰσότιμον in v.1 levels the audience: your faith equals ours — setting up the letter's egalitarian pastoral tone - The grace-and-peace wish (v.2) grounds everything in ἐπίγνωσις — already signaling the letter's key word

UNIT 2: Theological Indicative — What God Has Already Done (vv.3–4)

Function: Foundation layer; establishes the "already given" before issuing any commands

Argument structure:

SINCE [God's divine power has already granted everything needed for life and godliness]
  THROUGH [the knowledge of the one who called us]
  BY MEANS OF [his own glory and excellence]
THROUGH WHICH [he has granted the precious and magnificent promises]
  SO THAT [you may become partakers of the divine nature]
    HAVING ESCAPED [the world's corruption through desire]

Logical flow: This is a causal chain: - God's power → grants everything (v.3) - God's promises → enable participation in divine nature (v.4a) - That participation → requires escaping corruption (v.4b)

Key connectives: - Ὡς (causal): "since/inasmuch as" — grounds all following commands - δι' ὧν ("through which" v.4): the promises flow from the glory and excellence - ἵνα ("so that" v.4): purpose clause — the whole divine provision aims at participation in divine nature

Rhetorical note: vv.3–4 are a single subordinate clause with no main verb. The theological foundation is grammatically incomplete without the following imperative (v.5 ἐπιχορηγήσατε). This is deliberate: the indicative is incomplete without the imperative; God's provision is designed to elicit human response.

UNIT 3: Moral Imperative — The Sorites Chain (vv.5–7)

Function: The commanded response to the theological foundation

Argument structure:

FOR THIS VERY REASON (connecting back to vv.3–4):
  APPLYING ALL DILIGENCE,
    in your FAITH → supply VIRTUE
    in your VIRTUE → supply KNOWLEDGE
    in your KNOWLEDGE → supply SELF-CONTROL
    in your SELF-CONTROL → supply PERSEVERANCE
    in your PERSEVERANCE → supply GODLINESS
    in your GODLINESS → supply BROTHERLY LOVE
    in your BROTHERLY LOVE → supply LOVE

Rhetorical device — Sorites (climax/gradatio): The sorites is a figure of rhetorical accumulation where each term catches up the previous and adds to it. Classical examples in Stoic philosophy; the form signals: 1. Progressive moral development (not a checklist but a ladder) 2. Each virtue enables the next 3. The chain has both a foundation (faith) and a summit (love) 4. The chain is complete — love terminates it; there is no virtue beyond love

Structural observation: The chain begins with faith (the given, v.1's ἰσότιμον πίστιν) and ends with love (ἀγάπη). Faith and love are the two poles of Christian existence; the sorites describes the path between them. The intermediate virtues are not random — they trace a path from belief through character formation to community love.

Asymmetry in the chain: - Faith is given (received by divine allotment, v.1) - Virtue through love are commanded (supplied by human diligence) - This reflects the indicative/imperative structure: God's gift → human response

UNIT 4: Consequences — Two Paths (vv.8–11)

Function: The consequences of growth (v.8) and stagnation (v.9) followed by the ultimate promise (vv.10–11)

Argument structure (ABA' pattern):

A  v.8: IF these qualities exist and abound → FRUITFUL in knowledge of Christ
B  v.9: IF these qualities are absent → BLIND, NEARSIGHTED, FORGETFUL
A' vv.10–11: THEREFORE, diligently confirm → WILL NEVER STUMBLE → RICH WELCOME

Logical connectives: - γάρ (v.8): "for" — introduces the reason to pursue the sorites chain - γάρ (v.9): second "for" — the negative case - διό (v.10): "therefore" — inference drawn from vv.8–9: given both possible outcomes, choose the positive path - γάρ (v.11): "for" — introduces the promise that follows obedience - οὕτως (v.11): "in this way" — ties the promise to the obedience

Climax: v.11 is the structural climax of the first section (vv.1–11): "the entrance into the eternal kingdom will be abundantly supplied." The very verb echoes v.5 (ἐπιχορηγήσατε / ἐπιχορηγηθήσεται) — you supply now; God will supply then. This is a divine reciprocity embedded in the discourse structure.

UNIT 5: Apostolic Motivation — Peter's Testament (vv.12–15)

Function: Bridge between the theological section and the apologetic section; establishes the urgency of the letter

Argument structure:

THEREFORE (v.12) I will always remind you [even though you know and are established]
  REASON (v.13): it is right for me to stir you while I am alive
    REASON (v.14): I know my death is imminent (Christ told me)
THEREFORE (v.15) I will make sure you can remember after my departure

Rhetorical function: This unit is a captatio benevolentiae — a move to establish the speaker's goodwill and justify the letter's existence. Peter is not teaching them new doctrine; he is reminding them of what they know. The letter's authority derives not from novelty but from apostolic authentication of received truth.

Key discourse feature: The repetition of reminder vocabulary: - ὑπομιμνῄσκειν (v.12): "to remind" - ὑπομνήσει (v.13): "by reminder" - μνήμην ποιεῖσθαι (v.15): "to make remembrance"

The triple occurrence of memory/reminder terminology signals that the function of the entire letter is mnemonic — to fix established truth in memory against the coming distortion of false teaching.

UNIT 6: Apologetic Foundation — Eyewitness and Prophecy (vv.16–21)

Function: Establishes the epistemological foundation for the truth being reminded (vv.12–15)

Argument structure:

CLAIM: We did not follow myths when we proclaimed the power and coming of Christ (v.16a)
  GROUND 1: We were eyewitnesses of his majesty (v.16b)
    EVIDENCE: We received honor and glory — a voice from the Majestic Glory (v.17)
              "This is my Son, my Beloved, with whom I am well pleased"
    EVIDENCE: We heard this voice — we were there on the holy mountain (v.18)
  GROUND 2: We also have the prophetic word, made more certain (v.19a)
    EXHORTATION: Pay attention to it — as to a lamp in darkness (v.19b)
                 Until the day dawns and the morning star rises (v.19c)
HERMENEUTICAL PRINCIPLE: No prophecy of Scripture is one's own interpretation (v.20)
  REASON: Prophecy was never borne by human will (v.21a)
          But humans carried by the Holy Spirit spoke from God (v.21b)

Dual authority structure: The argument establishes two interlocking bases of authority: 1. Eyewitness testimony (vv.16–18): Apostolic first-hand experience of the Transfiguration 2. Prophetic Scripture (vv.19–21): The OT prophetic word, now confirmed and illuminated

These are not competing authorities but mutually reinforcing ones: the Transfiguration confirms the prophetic word; the prophetic word provides the interpretive framework for the Transfiguration.

Rhetorical device — Antithesis (v.16): οὐ γὰρ σεσοφισμένοις μύθοις...ἀλλ' ἐπόπται γενηθέντες

The not/but (οὐ...ἀλλά) antithesis is stark: - NOT: cleverly crafted myths (passive, manufactured, literary) - BUT: eyewitnesses (active, historical, firsthand)

The antithesis is reinforced by the contrast between σεσοφισμένοις (perfect passive — artificially crafted over time) and γενηθέντες (aorist — became, a specific event).

Rhetorical device — φέρω threefold echo (vv.17–21): The verb φέρω ("to bear/carry") appears in three forms across the passage: 1. v.17: ἐνεχθείσης (voice borne to Christ at Transfiguration) 2. v.18: ἐνεχθεῖσαν (that same voice heard by witnesses) 3. v.21: ἠνέχθη...φερόμενοι (prophecy not borne by human will; prophets borne by Spirit)

The verbal echo creates a theological argument through the discourse: the same divine "bearing" that carried the heavenly voice at the Transfiguration also carries the prophets in Scripture. Apostolic testimony (vv.16–18) and scriptural prophecy (vv.19–21) share a common divine mechanism. This is discourse-level apologetics: the authority of both rests on the same φέρω.

Inclusio and Structural Echoes

Chapter-level inclusio (vv.1–2 // vv.19–21):

  • vv.1–2: grace and peace "in the ἐπίγνωσις of God and Jesus our Lord"
  • vv.19–21: the prophetic word as the reliable lamp until the eschatological dawn

Both frame the content with the idea of knowing God correctly — through apostolic faith (v.1–2) and through prophetic Scripture (vv.19–21). The chapter begins and ends with epistemology.

ἐπιχορηγέω echo (v.5 // v.11):

  • v.5: ἐπιχορηγήσατε (supply abundantly — aorist active imperative, addressed to believers)
  • v.11: ἐπιχορηγηθήσεται (will be supplied abundantly — future passive, God as agent)

The echo structures the chapter: human obedience to supply the virtues → God's generous supply of the kingdom entrance. The believer's costly provision (like the choragos funding the chorus) is matched and exceeded by God's provision.

σπουδή / σπουδάζω echo:

  • v.5: πᾶσαν σπουδὴν παρεισενέγκαντες ("applying all diligence")
  • v.10: σπουδάσατε ("be diligent")
  • v.15: σπουδάσω ("I will be diligent")

The diligence motif frames both the command to believers (vv.5, 10) and Peter's own apostolic commitment (v.15). He commands what he practices.

ἐπίγνωσις frame:

  • v.2: grace and peace multiplied "in the full knowledge" (ἐπιγνώσει) of God and Christ
  • v.3: everything granted "through the full knowledge" (ἐπιγνώσεως) of the one who called
  • v.8: the chain keeps believers fruitful "in the full knowledge" (ἐπίγνωσιν) of Christ

The ἐπίγνωσις word frames the entire first theological section (vv.2–8), functioning as the letter's epistemological spine.

Paragraph and Sentence Cohesion

vv.3–4 (one sentence): The longest sentence in the chapter; the theological indicative is held in suspense until the main verb in v.4 (δεδώρηται). This grammatical length mirrors the theological richness: the foundation requires full elaboration before the command can be issued.

vv.5–7 (sorites): The anaphoric pattern (ἐν δὲ τῇ...τήν) creates rhythmic cohesion across three verses. The repetition is intentional — it is the form of the argument, not just its content.

vv.10–11 (conditional): The conditional structure (ταῦτα γὰρ ποιοῦντες → οὐ μὴ πταίσητέ ποτε → οὕτως ἐπιχορηγηθήσεται) is a three-step sequence: condition → promise → manner.

vv.20–21 (γάρ chain): v.21 is the explanation (γάρ) of v.20. The chain: - v.20 PRINCIPLE: prophecy is not one's own interpretation - v.21 REASON: because it was not borne by human will but by the Spirit

The discourse is tight: claim (v.20) → ground (v.21). This is the most compact and tightly argued unit in the chapter.
