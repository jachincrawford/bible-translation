Overview: The Vocabulary of 2 Peter 1

This chapter is lexically dense and distinctive. It contains six NT hapax legomena — words appearing nowhere else in the NT — and clusters several rare or theologically loaded terms in ways that signal deliberate compositional craft. The chapter's vocabulary falls into five major semantic clusters: knowledge, provision/supply, virtue/character, witness/testimony, and divine origin/inspiration.

Cluster 1: Knowledge — ἐπίγνωσις and γνῶσις

The most important lexical distinction in the chapter is between two Greek words for knowledge.

ἐπίγνωσις (Str. 1922) appears at [v.2], [v.3], and [v.8] (and again at 2:20 — four times in the letter). The ἐπί- prefix intensifies or directs the root γνῶσις: this is knowledge directed toward an object, full recognition, thorough acquaintance. In [v.2] it is the sphere in which grace and peace multiply; in [v.3] it is the channel through which God's power grants everything needed; in [v.8] it is the goal toward which the virtues keep the believer fruitful. ἐπίγνωσις in 2 Peter is never abstract — it is always the personal, directed knowledge of God and of Jesus Christ as Lord.

γνῶσις (Str. 1108) appears at [v.5] and [v.6] — exclusively within the sorites chain of virtues. Here it is the third rung of the ladder: something to be acquired through diligence, sandwiched between virtue and self-control. The contrast is deliberate: γνῶσις is practical wisdom, human discernment — a virtue to cultivate; ἐπίγνωσις is the relational, God-directed knowing that is the goal of the whole chapter. The false teachers of chapter 2 may have claimed superior γνῶσις; Peter places it in the middle of the virtue chain — valuable but not supreme.

Translation risk: Versions that translate both words as "knowledge" (without distinction) obscure a carefully constructed contrast between human wisdom and divine-relational knowing.

Cluster 2: Provision and Supply — δωρέομαι and ἐπιχορηγέω

The chapter builds an elaborate vocabulary of provision — God's giving and the believer's supplying — using two root families that create a structural echo across the whole chapter.

δωρέομαι (Str. 1433) appears twice in close succession. At [v.3] the perfect passive participle δεδωρημένης ("having been granted") describes the divine provision already made — everything needed for life and godliness. At [v.4] the perfect middle/deponent δεδώρηται ("he has granted") describes the promises given. The perfect tense in both cases is theologically significant: this is a completed, fully-made bestowal. The believer does not wait for divine resources; they have already been bestowed. The cognate noun δωρεά (free gift) underlies the word family — all this provision is gift-character, not reward.

ἐπιχορηγέω (Str. 2023) creates the chapter's most elegant structural echo. At [v.5] it appears as an aorist active imperative — ἐπιχορηγήσατε: "supply [abundantly]!" The command is addressed to believers. At [v.11] it appears as a future passive — ἐπιχορηγηθήσεται: "will be supplied abundantly." The subject is now God (divine passive), and the object is the believers' welcome into the eternal kingdom. The word's etymology deepens the echo: from χορηγός, the wealthy Athenian who funded the theater chorus at his own expense. The believer is called to be a lavish, costly funder of virtue in their own life [v.5]; God will be the lavish, costly funder of their eternal welcome [v.11]. Human costly provision → divine costly provision.

Cluster 3: Virtue and Character — The Sorites Vocabulary

The eight terms of the sorites chain ([vv.5–7]) are individually significant and collectively form a lexical argument.

πίστις (Str. 4102) — faith. The foundation, not the achievement: received by divine allotment ([v.1], λαγχάνω) and already held by the readers before the chain begins. Faith is the given; everything else is the response.

ἀρετή (Str. 703) — virtue/excellence/moral goodness. Appears twice: at [v.3] of God's own excellence (the LXX sense — praiseworthy acts/attributes), and at [v.5] of the human virtue to be added to faith. This double use is not careless — it implies that human virtue is a participation in and reflection of divine virtue. The word has deep roots in both Stoic philosophy (excellence proper to a thing) and LXX theology (God's praiseworthy self-manifestations).

γνῶσις (Str. 1108) — knowledge/wisdom (see Cluster 1 above).

ἐγκράτεια (Str. 1466) — self-control. A Stoic cardinal virtue — the mastery of one's impulses and desires by reason and will. Peter Christianizes the Stoic vocabulary: self-control appears in Galatians 5:23 as a fruit of the Spirit. The presence of this word signals that Peter is engaging Hellenistic moral vocabulary deliberately.

ὑπομονή (Str. 5281) — endurance/perseverance. Endurance under circumstances (contrast μακροθυμία, patience with persons). Often translated "patience" in older versions, obscuring this distinction. Endurance is the virtue that holds when external pressure is greatest.

εὐσέβεια (Str. 2150) — godliness/piety. Appears at [v.3] (the goal of divine provision), [v.6] (a virtue of the chain), [v.7] (foundation for brotherly love). Four times in the chapter, including 3:11 — it is 2 Peter's signature virtue word. In Hellenistic culture, εὐσέβεια was a civic virtue — proper reverence toward gods, elders, and community. Peter adopts and Christianizes it: godliness as the whole orientation of life toward God.

φιλαδελφία (Str. 5360) — brotherly love/affection. The penultimate virtue: the warm, family-bond love specific to the believing community. The movement from εὐσέβεια (vertical — toward God) through φιλαδελφία (horizontal — toward fellow believers) to ἀγάπη (universal) traces a logical moral arc.

ἀγάπη (Str. 26) — love. The crown and terminus of the chain. Distinguished from φιλαδελφία (sibling-love) and from all Hellenistic love-vocabulary: ἀγάπη is willed, outgoing, self-giving love regardless of the worthiness of the recipient. No virtue follows it; it is the goal, not a rung.

Cluster 4: Witness and Testimony — ἐπόπτης, μῦθος, παρουσία

The apologetic section ([vv.16–18]) deploys a specific vocabulary of testimony and truth-claim.

μῦθος (Str. 3454) — myth/tale/story. In philosophical usage, μῦθος is the opposite of λόγος (reasoned argument). Every use in the NT is pejorative (1 Tim 1:4; 4:7; 2 Tim 4:4; Tit 1:14). The compound σεσοφισμένοις μύθοις ("cleverly devised myths") at [v.16] emphasizes the crafted, manufactured quality of the false teaching — they are not honest mistakes but deliberate fabrications.

ἐπόπτης (Str. 2030) — eyewitness/highest-grade initiate. A rare and charged word. In the mystery religions, the ἐπόπτης was the initiate who had completed the highest level of initiation and witnessed the final secret rites. Peter applies this word to the apostles: we are not mystery-religion initiates who paid for a manufactured experience — we are genuine ἐπόπται of Christ's actual glory [v.16]. The polemical subversion is intentional: what mystery religions promised by initiation, the apostles received by historical witness.

παρουσία (Str. 3952) — coming/arrival/presence. In the Hellenistic world, the official royal visit of a king or emperor to a city. By early Christian usage, it is the technical term for Christ's Second Coming. At [v.16], it is paired with δύναμιν (power) — "the power and coming of our Lord Jesus Christ." The Transfiguration functions as a preview of that Parousia: the same glory, the same divine voice, the same overwhelming greatness that will characterize the Second Coming was made visible on the holy mountain [vv.17–18].

Cluster 5: Divine Origin and Inspiration — φέρω and ἐπίλυσις

The chapter's final movement ([vv.19–21]) contains two of its most lexically significant features.

φέρω (Str. 5342) — to bear/carry/bring. The most structurally important verb in the chapter appears three times in its final movement, creating a deliberate literary argument through repeated vocabulary.

At [v.17], ἐνεχθείσης (aorist passive participle) describes the divine voice "having been borne" to Christ at the Transfiguration — the voice was not self-generated but carried from the divine presence to the Son.

At [v.18], ἐνεχθεῖσαν (aorist passive participle again, different case) describes the same voice "borne from heaven" — the same event from the eyewitnesses' perspective.

At [v.21], the verb appears twice: ἠνέχθη (aorist passive indicative) — "prophecy was never borne by human will"; and φερόμενοι (present passive participle) — "being carried along by the Holy Spirit." The present tense of the participle is significant: while the speaking (ἐλάλησαν, aorist) was a completed act, the being-carried was a continuous, ongoing condition during which the speaking occurred.

The threefold φέρω creates a theological argument through lexical repetition: the same divine "bearing" that carried the voice at the Transfiguration also "carries" the prophets in Scripture. Apostolic eyewitness and scriptural prophecy share a common mechanism — divine φέρω.

ἐπίλυσις (Str. 1955) — NT hapax legomenon. Appears only here in the NT [v.20]. From ἐπι- + λύω (to loose/unloose) — to loosen upon, to unpack, to interpret or explain. The verbal cognate ἐπιλύω appears in Mark 4:34 (Jesus "explained" the parables to his disciples). The crux of v.20 turns on who "one's own" (ἰδίας) refers to. Lexically, γίνεται + ἐπιλύσεως (genitive of source) = "comes from interpretation/unloosing." The context of v.21 (prophets were carried by the Spirit, not by human will) strongly supports the authorial sense: the prophecy did not originate from the prophet's own interpretation of things. The hapax status of the word means no intra-NT parallel exists to confirm this reading; the verbal cognate and the immediate context must carry the weight.

Six hapax legomena in twenty-one verses is an unusually high density. This is one of several features that make 2 Peter lexically distinctive within the NT canon — along with its unusually Hellenistic vocabulary (ἐπόπτης, εὐσέβεια, θεῖος, ἀρετή, ἐγκράτεια) and its highly periodic sentence structures.

Signature Word: ἐπίγνωσις as Structural Spine

The word ἐπίγνωσις deserves a final summary note because it functions as the chapter's structural spine. It appears at three critical junctures:

  • [v.2]: Grace and peace multiply in the full knowledge of God and Christ — the epistemological ground of Christian experience
  • [v.3]: Everything needed for life and godliness granted through the full knowledge of the one who called — the mechanism of divine provision
  • [v.8]: The virtue chain keeps believers fruitful in the full knowledge of our Lord Jesus Christ — the goal of all moral effort

The chapter begins with ἐπίγνωσις as the ground of blessing, moves through it as the channel of provision, and ends with it as the goal of virtue. Knowing God — deeply, personally, precisely — is not one topic among many in this chapter. It is the axis on which everything else turns.
