The Question of Authorship: A History of the Debate

No other letter in the NT canon has faced more sustained questions about authorship than 2 Peter. The history of the church's engagement with this text is inseparable from the history of that debate, and responsible historical scholarship must address it before turning to interpretation.

The ancient testimony is divided. Origen (c. 185–253) is the earliest to note the dispute explicitly: "Peter has left one acknowledged epistle; perhaps also a second, for it is doubted." (Commentary on John 5.3). Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 260–339) lists 2 Peter among the antilegomena — the "disputed" books — saying that "it is not canonical, yet since it has seemed useful to many, it has been studied along with the other Scriptures" (Ecclesiastical History 3.3.1–4). Jerome notes the stylistic difference from 1 Peter and acknowledges the tradition that 2 Peter was written by a different hand (perhaps a secretary) to account for the variation.

The canonical acceptance was gradual. 2 Peter is absent from Marcion's canon (but Marcion excluded much), absent from the Muratorian Fragment (though the Fragment's text may be incomplete), and slow to appear in the earliest patristic quotation chains. By contrast, Clement of Alexandria quoted it, and Athanasius included it in his canonical list of 367 AD. The Council of Hippo (393) and Carthage (397) confirmed it as canonical. By the late fourth century, the Western church had largely settled the question in favor of canonicity.

The Reformation revisited the question. Erasmus raised doubts about Petrine authorship on stylistic grounds in the sixteenth century. Calvin, characteristically, accepted the letter's authority while acknowledging the stylistic question: "As to the difference in style, it is quite evident that this epistle was written in a rougher style than the former; but as the substance is similar, I am inclined to think that this was owing to Peter's using a different amanuensis. For it was customary for great men to have secretaries who wrote for them." (Calvin's Commentaries on 2 Peter). Luther accepted the canonical status of the letter but placed it among the "contested" works.

The modern critical consensus since the 19th century has generally favored pseudonymity — a later author writing in Peter's name as a recognized literary convention (cf. Bauckham, Jude, 2 Peter, WBC, 1983). Arguments include: stylistic differences from 1 Peter, late vocabulary, apparent dependence on Jude, the apparent post-apostolic concern with the delay of the Parousia. Against this, a minority of scholars (Green, Kruger, Davids) have defended authenticity: Semitic name form, independent Transfiguration tradition, coherent historical setting in the early 60s, and the argument that a pseudonymous author would not have created more stylistic variation from 1 Peter than less.

The history of this debate is significant for the chapter's reception: communities that resolved the authorship question toward authentic Petrine origin read the chapter with the weight of last-apostolic-words; communities more open to pseudonymity read it as authoritative Christian tradition regardless of the named author. Both have found in the chapter a reliable theological resource.

The Patristic Reading: Theosis and Moral Formation

The phrase "partakers of the divine nature" [v.4] became, in the hands of the Greek patristic theologians, one of the foundational texts for the doctrine of theosis (deification) — the teaching that the ultimate destiny of the redeemed is genuine participation in God's own life.

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 130–200) anticipates the theosis reading in his famous formulation: "The Word of God, our Lord Jesus Christ, who did, through his transcendent love, become what we are, that he might bring us to be even what he himself is" (Against Heresies 5, Preface). Though Irenaeus does not quote 2 Peter 1:4 here, the conceptual structure is identical: the Incarnation enables humanity's participation in divine life.

Athanasius (c. 296–373) gives the classic Eastern formulation: "He was made man that we might be made God" (On the Incarnation 54.3) — and elsewhere cites 2 Peter 1:4 explicitly in the context of arguing that the Son must be truly divine if believers are to genuinely participate in divinity (Against the Arians 1.9). Athanasius's logic: you cannot genuinely share in a nature that is itself created. The deification of believers requires an uncreated divine nature to participate in. 2 Peter 1:4 thus became a proof-text in the Arian controversy, where it was used to establish the eternal divinity of the Son.

Maximus the Confessor (c. 580–662) developed the most sophisticated patristic account of theosis with sustained reference to 2 Peter 1:4. For Maximus, participation in divine nature does not mean sharing in God's essence (which remains utterly beyond creatures) but in God's energies — his self-communicating activities of grace, life, and love. This distinction (later formalized by Gregory Palamas in the 14th century) protected the Creator-creature distinction while affirming the genuine reality of the participation. Maximus reads the sorites chain of [vv.5–7] as the path of praktike (practical virtue) leading toward theoria (contemplative union) — a reading that influenced the whole Eastern ascetic tradition.

John Chrysostom preached extensively on 2 Peter, with particular attention to the virtue chain [vv.5–7]. His homilies treat the sorites as a practical program of moral formation accessible to ordinary Christians — not the preserve of ascetics. His reading emphasizes the community dimensions of the virtues: φιλαδελφία and ἀγάπη are not private spiritual states but observable communal realities. Chrysostom's pastoral reading has influenced homiletical tradition in both East and West.

Medieval Reception: Allegory, Scholasticism, and Mysticism

The Western medieval tradition read 2 Peter 1 less through the theosis lens and more through the lens of moral formation and eschatological warning. The lamp of [v.19] featured frequently in illumination theology — Scripture as the sufficient light for the church's pilgrimage through the "dark place" of history. Thomas Aquinas in his commentary on 2 Peter treats [vv.3–4] as a statement of grace preceding merit: God's provision comes first; virtue is the grateful response. His reading is Augustinian in structure and carefully guards the priority of divine grace.

The mystical tradition appropriated the "partakers of the divine nature" language with varying levels of orthodox circumspection. Meister Eckhart (c. 1260–1328) pushed the participatory language toward a kind of mystical identity that was posthumously condemned. John of the Cross (1542–1591) used the participation language more carefully, treating union with God as the transformation of the will by love — never an ontological fusion — and [v.4] as the scriptural foundation for the soul's ascent toward God in caridad (divine love).

The Reformation: Authority, Scripture, and the Text

The Reformers' engagement with 2 Peter 1 clustered around two themes: the authority of Scripture (vv.19–21) and the relationship of grace and works in the virtue chain (vv.3–11).

Luther read [vv.19–21] in the context of his primary Reformation concern: the authority of Scripture over against tradition and papal pronouncement. The text that "no prophecy of Scripture comes from someone's own interpretation" was read (against its original authorial sense) as a warning against individual interpretation by the Pope or councils — Scripture interprets itself (analogia scripturae), not by the magisterium's authority. This was a polemical appropriation of the text in service of sola scriptura. Whatever Peter meant by ἰδίας ἐπιλύσεως, Luther's reading of the passage anchored the Reformation's epistemological claim.

Calvin was more careful in his exegetical approach. His commentary on [v.20] explicitly argues for the authorial sense — the prophets did not produce their own interpretation, because the Spirit was the source — while also drawing the implication for church authority: if prophecy is not of private interpretation by its very origin, it cannot be made subject to any human interpretive authority, including Rome's. The exegesis and the polemical use are more carefully distinguished in Calvin than in Luther.

The Reformers and the virtue chain [vv.5–11]: The sorites was carefully read through the lens of sola gratia. [vv.3–4] — the complete divine provision — were read as establishing grace's priority; [vv.5–11] — the human supply of virtues — were read as sanctification, not meritorious works. The distinction between justification (received, not achieved, [v.1] — λαγχάνω) and sanctification (the active moral response of [vv.5–7]) structured the Reformation reading of the whole passage.

The Modern Critical Era: Questions and Recoveries

The historical-critical tradition (from Schleiermacher through Baur and the Tübingen school to the 20th century) approached 2 Peter primarily as a document to be dated and sourced rather than interpreted theologically. The late dating (mid-second century in some accounts) meant that the chapter's claims to Petrine eyewitness [vv.16–18] were treated as pious fiction — the letter was read as a pseudonymous appropriation of apostolic authority in service of early catholic concerns about emerging Gnosticism. This reading shaped much of 20th-century NT scholarship.

The recovery of rhetorical analysis (Kennedy, Witherington, Neyrey) has reoriented scholarly attention toward the text's actual argument rather than its historical origins. Jerome Neyrey's commentary (2 Peter, Jude, Anchor Bible, 1993) reads the chapter through Greco-Roman rhetoric and household codes, illuminating how the text would have functioned in its social world. Richard Bauckham's influential WBC commentary (1983) remains the most significant modern critical work, arguing for deliberate pseudonymous composition as a recognized and honorable form — "testament fiction" — while affirming the letter's theological integrity.

Evangelical scholarship in the late 20th and early 21st centuries has invested significant effort in the authorship question. Michael Green (2 Peter and Jude, TNTC, 1987), Thomas Schreiner (1, 2 Peter, Jude, NAC, 2003), and Peter Davids (The Letters of 2 Peter and Jude, PNTC, 2006) each defend authenticity or early tradition while engaging the critical objections seriously.

Ecumenical convergence on theosis: Perhaps the most significant development in recent decades is the convergence of Eastern Orthodox, Roman Catholic, and some Protestant scholarship on the doctrine of participation in divine nature. A new generation of Protestant theologians (Michael Gorman, Veli-Matti Kärkkäinen) has argued that theosis is not exclusively Eastern but is present in Paul, John, and 2 Peter — and that recovering this participatory soteriology enriches the sometimes juridical (guilt/forgiveness) structures dominant in Western Protestant theology. 2 Peter 1:4 has become a locus for ecumenical dialogue and theological retrieval.

The Transfiguration Account: History of a Crux

The slight differences between 2 Peter 1:17's version of the divine voice and the Synoptic Gospels' accounts [vv.17–18] have been discussed by virtually every significant commentator.

Early harmonizing solutions (Augustine, Aquinas) treated the differences as complementary accounts of the same event from different witnesses or traditions, requiring no resolution — Scripture's diversity is its richness.

Historical-critical approaches have often treated the differences as evidence against authenticity: a pseudonymous author should have harmonized more carefully with the known Gospel tradition. Defenders of authenticity argue the opposite: a pseudonymous author would have harmonized; the variation is the mark of independent memory.

The verbal form debate: The present active "making known" (ἐγνωρίσαμεν [v.16]) versus the Gospels' narrative past tense has been read as evidence of either: (a) Peter's personal recollection in first-person pastoral retrospect, or (b) a post-Easter liturgical usage in which the Transfiguration was "made known" in preaching. The debate touches on the question of how early Christian communities used their traditions — as historical record or as living proclamation.

Enduring Interpretive Traditions

The chapter has generated several durable interpretive traditions that have shaped Christian formation across centuries:

  1. Eastern Orthodox hesychasm: The sorites chain [vv.5–7] and the participation in divine nature [v.4] became foundational for the hesychast tradition of contemplative prayer and virtue formation, particularly in the work of Gregory Palamas who developed the essence/energies distinction to protect both divine transcendence and genuine participation.

  2. Western ascetic tradition: The lamp simile [v.19] and the testimony of the departing apostle [vv.12–15] have shaped monastic formation literature — the idea that the religious life is the deliberate navigation of a dark world by the steady light of Scripture, awaiting the eschatological dawn.

  3. Protestant bibliology: [vv.19–21] are among the most cited texts in Protestant treatments of biblical inspiration and authority. The formulation of "men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit" [v.21] has been foundational for evangelical doctrine of Scripture, with the φερόμενοι participle especially influential in articulating a concursive view of inspiration.

  4. Ecumenical participation theology: [v.4] has become a meeting point for Eastern, Catholic, and Protestant theologies of salvation — the one text in the NT where "participation in divine nature" is explicitly stated, it invites theological dialogue that the more juridical texts (Romans, Galatians) do not naturally generate.

The history of the chapter's interpretation is a history of the church arguing over its most important questions: Who is Christ? What is salvation? How does Scripture function? What is the Christian life? That a single chapter of 21 verses has been at the center of these debates for nearly two millennia is itself a testimony to the theological density that Peter — or his school — achieved in this remarkable text.
