> **KEY TERMS — v.1:**
> - **pseudodidaskaloi** [NT-HAPAX] — ψευδοδιδάσκαλοι: "false teachers." Only occurrence in NT. Compound of pseudēs (false) + didaskalos (teacher). Cf. pseudoprophetai (false prophets) in same verse — deliberate parallelism.
> - **pareisaxousin** — παρεισάξουσιν: from παρεισάγω (pareisago). "Bring in alongside/secretly." Prefix para + eis + ago. The "smuggling" nuance of covert introduction is load-bearing.
> - **haireseis** — αἱρέσεις: from αἵρεσις. "Heresies" here means destructive factions/sects; the word originally meant "choice" or "school of thought." In context it is modified by apoleias (of destruction) — "heresies of destruction."
> - **apoleias** — ἀπωλείας (occurrence 1 of 3 in ch. 2): gen-sg. Track: v.1 (haireseis apoleias), v.1 again (tachinon apoleian), v.3 (he apoleia auton). A dominant motif.
> - **agorasanta** — ἀγοράσαντα: from ἀγοράζω. Redemption/purchase term. The Master "bought" (purchased/redeemed) these false teachers — they deny the one who owns them. Strong market/ransom resonance.
> - **despoten** — δεσπότης: "Master/Owner/Sovereign." Not the common kyrios (Lord) but the absolute-ownership term. Intensifies the scandal of the denial.
> - **tachinon** — ταχινήν: "swift/imminent." Same word used in 1:14 of Peter's own imminent death (NOTE: lexical echo with 1:14 — tachinē there of Peter's departure; here of the false teachers' destruction).
> - **apoleian** (occurrence 2 of 3 in ch. 2) — verse-final, emphatic position.

> **KEY TERMS — v.4:**
> - **tartarosas** — ταρταρώσας [NT-HAPAX]: from ταρταρόω. "Having cast into Tartarus." Only NT occurrence. Tartarus is the Greek mythological underworld prison for the Titans — here appropriated for the realm of imprisoned angels. Peter's use of Greco-Roman cosmological vocabulary is culturally significant.
> - **seirois/seirais** — σειροῖς/σειραῖς [VARIANT noted above]: "pits" or "chains." The ambiguity is not merely textual but theological — confinement vs. binding.
> - **zophou** — ζόφου: from ζόφος. "Darkness/gloom." Not the common skotos but the Homeric/poetic term for pitch darkness. Appears again in v.17 (zophos tou skotous — "gloom of darkness").
> - **tereo** — τηρέω (teroumenous): "being-kept/reserved." The divine custody motif. The keeping is ongoing (present participle) — they are currently held for future judgment. Verb echoes 1:19 and recurs in v.9 and v.17.

> - **tephrostas** — τεφρώσας [NT-HAPAX]: from τεφρόω. "Having reduced to ashes." Only NT occurrence. The catastrophic finality is encoded in the term itself — not merely burned but turned to ash/cinders. Strong volcanic imagery (cf. the region's geology).

> **KEY TERMS — v.11:**
> - **ischyi kai dynamei meizonas** — ἰσχύϊ καὶ δυνάμει μείζονες: "greater in strength and power." The angels' superiority to the false teachers is underscored — and yet even they do not bring a blasphemous charge. The argument is a fortiori: if the mighty restrain themselves, how much more should these presumptuous men.
> - **pherousin** — φέρουσιν: from φέρω. "Bring/bear." NOTE: φέρω echo from ch. 1 — the same verb used of the prophetic word "borne along" by the Holy Spirit (1:21, pheromenoi). > - **blasphemon krisin** — βλάσφημον κρίσιν: "blasphemous judgment/charge." The very thing the false teachers do without restraint (v.10), angels refuse to do even against those they could legitimately condemn. Striking reversal.

> **KEY TERMS — v.22:**
> - **alethous paroimias** — ἀληθοῦς παροιμίας: "the true proverb." The proverb is introduced as genuinely true (alethous — "true/real"). Two proverbs are cited — the first from Prov 26:11 (dog returning to vomit), the second from an unidentified source (the washed pig).
> - **Kyon** — κύων: "dog." In ancient Jewish thought, a highly pejorative term. The dog "returns to its own vomit" (Prov 26:11 LXX: kuon epistrepsas epi ton heautou emetou) — Peter adapts/echoes the LXX closely.
> - **exerama** — ἐξέραμα: "vomit." From exerao. A vivid and deliberately coarse term — the thing expelled from the body returned to. Cf. Prov 26:11 (LXX emetos = vomit). The exerama here (only NT occurrence of this specific form) emphasizes the repulsive nature of the return.
> - **Hys** — ὗς: "sow/pig." Female swine. The second proverb. "A sow having washed, to wallowing in mud." The washing (bathing) was the moment of cleansing — the baptism echo is widely noted in the scholarly tradition. The sow returns to what is natural to her.
> - **borborou** — βορβόρου: "of mud/filth/mire." Gen-sg from βόρβορος. The destination of the washed pig — the mire from which she came. The final word of the chapter. Thematically, it answers the opening — those brought in by false teachers, those who seemed to escape (v.20), those who knew the way of righteousness (v.21) — they return to the βόρβορος. The chapter ends in the mire.
> - NOTE: The double proverb (dog + pig) forms a deliberate rhetorical climax: two animals, two returns to their respective pollution — complete and unreserved apostasy. Cf. the closing structure of the Sermon (Matt 7) with its contrasting pairs.
