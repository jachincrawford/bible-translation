The same thing that happened in the Old Testament is happening right now in your community. [vv.1–2] Back then, there were false prophets mixed in among God's people. Today, there are false teachers mixed in among you — and they are dangerous precisely because they blend in. They aren't announcing themselves as enemies of the faith. Instead, they are quietly introducing ideas that will destroy it from the inside. They even go so far as to deny the very Master who bought them with his own life. And the damage doesn't stop with them: many people are following their lead, living however they please, and dragging the reputation of the truth through the mud along the way. [v.3] What drives these teachers, underneath all the spiritual-sounding language, is simple greed. They have made up their message to exploit the people who trust them. But here is something they don't realize: the verdict on them has existed since before they even started. God's judgment isn't sleeping. It has been building, and it is coming.

How can we be sure? History tells us. [vv.4–9] Look at three examples Peter draws from the ancient record. First, God did not let the angels who sinned go unpunished — he cast them into the darkest confinement, where they wait for the day of judgment. Second, God did not look the other way when the ancient world filled itself with wickedness — he brought the flood and swept it away, though not before protecting Noah, a man who kept preaching what was right, along with seven others. Third, God did not let Sodom and Gomorrah stand — he burned those cities to ash and made them a permanent warning to everyone who would come after. But again, he rescued Lot first. Lot was a righteous man who lived in those cities and was tormented day after day by everything he saw and heard around him. The point of all three examples is the same: God knows exactly what he is doing. He knows how to rescue people who belong to him out of whatever they are going through, and he knows how to keep the guilty under judgment until the day he has set for it.

These false teachers fall into that second category — and Peter wants his readers to see them clearly. [vv.10–14] They live to satisfy their own cravings, and they despise anyone who has authority over them. They are bold enough to speak contemptuously about spiritual powers that even the mightiest angels approach with caution. They are like animals that exist only on instinct — and like those animals, destruction is where they are headed. Their pleasure is to party in broad daylight, shamelessly. They come to your shared meals and feast alongside you while secretly rotting the community from within. Their eyes never stop hunting for the next opportunity. They are especially skilled at identifying people who are not yet settled in their faith and dragging them off course. At the core of everything they do is greed — a greed so practiced it has become second nature.

The model for all of this is an old one. [vv.15–16] These teachers have abandoned the straight path — the same way a prophet named Balaam once abandoned it, because he loved what money could buy more than he loved what was right. In Balaam's case, God rebuked him through the most improbable possible messenger: a donkey spoke in plain human language and stopped a prophet in his tracks. The absurdity of it was the point. When a man is chasing money harder than truth, even the voice of a donkey can see it more clearly than he can.

The promises these teachers make are just as empty as they are. [vv.17–19] Peter reaches for two images: they are like wells you walk to in desperate thirst and find completely dry; like morning mist that looks promising and then the wind takes it away. Whatever they are offering evaporates on contact. Meanwhile, the deepest darkness is what awaits them. They flatter and inflate people with impressive-sounding words, specifically targeting those who are just in the process of leaving their old lives behind — new and still vulnerable. They offer freedom, but the offer is a lie. They themselves are enslaved to the very corruption they are peddling. Because that is always how it works: whatever has defeated you is what owns you.

The most sobering part of all of this comes at the end — the tragedy of those who once knew the truth. [vv.20–22] If someone has genuinely escaped the corruption of this world through knowing Jesus Christ, and then turns around, gets tangled up in it again, and is overcome — that person is in a worse position than if they had never known at all. That is a hard truth, but it is real. It would have been better for them never to have encountered the way of righteousness than to have learned it, embraced it, and then walked away from it. Peter closes with two unflinching images from common life: a dog going back to eat what it already vomited up, and a pig that has just been washed heading straight back to roll in the mud. The point is not to be cruel — it is to be honest. The nature underneath has not changed, and that is the real danger when someone who has known the truth turns back to what they left.
