## Overview

Second Peter 2 has generated a remarkably continuous interpretive tradition across twenty centuries — not despite its difficulties, but because of them. Its three exemplary judgments [vv.4–8], its enigmatic δόξας crux [v.10], its devastating portrait of apostasy [vv.20–22], and the figure of Balaam [vv.15–16] have each attracted concentrated theological attention. This essay traces the major voices era by era, without adjudicating between them.

---

## Early Church (Pre-325)

**Origen and the Tartarus Question [v.4]**

No early interpreter engaged v.4 more distinctively than Origen of Alexandria (c. 185–254). The hapax ταρταρόω — "cast into Tartarus" — invited serious theological attention, and Origen brought to it his characteristic preoccupation with the pre-existence and fall of spiritual beings. In the *De Principiis*, Origen develops the view that the fallen angels of v.4 represent rational souls (*logikoi*) who pre-existed in a prior state of spiritual equality with God and fell through misuse of freedom. The imprisonment in Tartarus, for Origen, is not necessarily permanent: consistent with his broader universalism (*apokatastasis*), even these fallen ones may ultimately be restored through remedial punishment. This reading was substantially out of step with the emerging consensus; Origen's angelology and universalism were condemned at the Second Council of Constantinople (553). But his engagement with v.4 established the questions later interpreters would have to answer: What exactly is Tartarus? When did the angelic sin occur? Is the judgment final?

**Clement of Alexandria on False Teachers**

Clement (c. 150–215) did not produce a running commentary on 2 Peter, but he cited and alluded to chapter 2 in the *Stromata* in the context of distinguishing true gnosis from its counterfeit. For Clement, the false teachers of [v.1] exemplify a pattern he identified in his own setting: teachers who cloak morally destructive instruction in the language of spiritual liberation. The warning that many will follow "licentious ways" [v.2] and that the "way of truth will be blasphemed" maps directly onto Clement's polemic against libertine Gnostic groups — particularly those who used spiritual language to sanction sexual license. Clement reads the chapter as apostolic warrant for discriminating between legitimate and counterfeit Christian teaching.

**Petrine Authorship in Early Canon Discussion**

The chapter's canonical reception was shadowed from the beginning by authorship uncertainty. Origen, the first to cite 2 Peter by name, is also the first to flag the uncertainty: "Peter has left one acknowledged epistle; perhaps also a second, for it is doubted" (*Commentary on John* 5.3). Eusebius of Caesarea places 2 Peter among the *antilegomena* — acknowledged but disputed books — noting that while many find it useful, its canonicity was not universally settled (*Ecclesiastical History* 3.3.1–4). The stylistic distance from 1 Peter, visible to ancient readers as to modern ones, was the primary concern. The practical effect on chapter 2 specifically: communities uncertain about the letter's authority read its stern warnings with somewhat more caution; communities that accepted it fully read v.1's "there will also be false teachers among you" as apostolic prediction bearing full canonical weight.

## Nicene and Post-Nicene Interpretation

**Augustine on Vv.20–22 and the Question of Genuine vs. Nominal Faith**

Augustine of Hippo (354–430) returned repeatedly to vv.20–22 in his anti-Donatist and anti-Pelagian contexts, and the passage became load-bearing for his theology of grace and perseverance. The figure of the person who has "escaped the defilements of the world through the knowledge of our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ" and then returns to entanglement [v.20] raised an acute question: was such a person ever genuinely regenerate?

Augustine's answer, developed across several works including the *De Correptione et Gratia*, is nuanced: some who exhibit outward Christian behavior and even enjoy real benefits of association with the church have not received the gift of perseverance, which belongs only to the elect. Their eventual apostasy, while genuinely a falling-away from what they had known, does not overturn the security of those who are truly God's. The proverbs of v.22 — the dog and the sow — confirm for Augustine not that regeneration can be lost but that the returning apostate reveals a nature that was never truly transformed. The sow was washed; she was not changed. This reading, set against Pelagian voluntarism and Donatist perfectionism alike, shaped Western theology's reading of the passage for a millennium.

**Jerome and the Balaam Passage**

Jerome (c. 347–420) engaged the Balaam material [vv.15–16] in his commentaries and letters primarily as a warning to clergy. In the *Epistula* and in his preface to commentaries on the prophets, Jerome invokes Balaam as the type of the hireling prophet — the man who sells spiritual authority for financial advantage. Jerome's context was the visible corruption of clergy in the late imperial church, and he found in Peter's use of Balaam an apostolic license to name that corruption plainly. The detail that a voiceless animal had to rebuke the prophet [v.16] was, for Jerome, a pointed humiliation: when clergy pursue gain, they are morally outclassed by beasts. Jerome also shows awareness of the broader Balaam tradition in Jewish sources, noting that the figure carried a weight of villainy beyond what the Numbers narrative alone supplies.

## Medieval Interpretation

**Aquinas on V.1 and the Scope of Redemption**

The phrase "denying the Master who bought them" [v.1] — with its explicit purchase-language (ἀγοράσαντα αὐτοὺς) — made v.1 a locus for medieval debate over the extent of Christ's redemption. Thomas Aquinas (*Summa Theologiae* III.q.46–49 and in his New Testament lecture series) addressed the question of whether Christ's atonement was universal or particular. The false teachers of v.1 are described as denying the Master who *bought them* — which suggests that even those who ultimately perish were, in some sense, purchased by Christ's death. Aquinas distinguished between the *sufficiency* of the atonement (infinite, extending to all) and its *efficiency* (applied to those who believe). Christ "bought" the false teachers in the sense that his death was sufficient to redeem them; their denial negates the application, not the sufficiency. This framework, which became standard in Scholastic soteriology, allowed Aquinas to honor the universalizing force of "bought them" without conceding universal salvation.

**The False Teacher Warnings and Medieval Heresy**

Chapter 2's warnings were regularly deployed in medieval ecclesiastical rhetoric against movements condemned as heretical — Cathars, Waldensians, Flagellants, and others. The figure of the false teacher who "secretly introduces" [v.1] destructive teaching, attracts followers [v.2], promises freedom while being a slave of corruption [v.19], and ends worse than he began [v.20] was treated as a prophetic template. Medieval commentators, including those in the Glossa Ordinaria tradition, generally read chapter 2 as predictive: the apostle foresaw the heresies of subsequent centuries and warned against them. The chapter thus became a rhetorical resource for inquisitorial and polemical literature, its warnings absorbed into the institutional church's self-authorization to name and suppress deviance.

## The Reformation

**Calvin on Vv.1 and 20–22**

John Calvin's commentary on 2 Peter (1551) is among the most careful Reformation-era treatments. On v.1's "buying" language, Calvin steers between the Scholastic sufficiency/efficiency distinction and the more radical reading that Christ purchased all persons without exception. Calvin holds that the false teachers had been "bought" in the sense that they received an outward participation in the covenant community and its privileges — including baptism and the external hearing of the gospel — but were never truly united to Christ. Their "buying" was real at the level of visible church membership, not at the level of effectual redemption. This reading, which would influence later Reformed covenant theology, allowed Calvin to maintain both the specificity of saving grace and the seriousness of apostasy.

On vv.20–22, Calvin's reading closely parallels Augustine but draws the distinctively Reformed inference about assurance. The passage does not threaten the genuine believer, because those who truly know Christ through faith cannot ultimately fall away. The persons described in vv.20–21 had an "external" or "incomplete" knowledge — the Greek ἐπίγνωσις notwithstanding. Calvin's handling is exegetically strained at points, but the theological motive is clear: to maintain both the warning's seriousness and the safety of authentic faith.

**Luther on Freedom and Slavery [v.19]**

Luther found in v.19 — "promising them freedom while they themselves are slaves of corruption" — a compressed statement of a theme central to his theology. The irony of the freedom-enslaved teacher maps directly onto Luther's analysis of the human condition under works-righteousness: those who promise liberation through moral achievement are themselves enslaved to the very self that religion is meant to transcend. Luther did not write a sustained commentary on 2 Peter, but the Reformation dynamic between true evangelical freedom and false libertine freedom drew on this passage. In Luther's reading, the false teachers of chapter 2 represent not merely immoral persons but a structural religious deception: the language of freedom in the service of bondage.

## Modern Critical Scholarship

**The Jude–2 Peter Relationship**

The dominant question in modern critical scholarship on 2 Peter 2 has been its literary relationship to the Epistle of Jude. The verbal parallels between Jude 4–18 and 2 Peter 2:1–3:3 are extensive and precise enough that literary dependence in one direction or the other is near-universally accepted; independent common tradition is a minority position. Richard Bauckham, in what remains the most influential modern commentary (*Jude, 2 Peter*, WBC, 1983), argues that 2 Peter is dependent on Jude rather than vice versa: 2 Peter expands, explains, and in places systematizes material from Jude; Jude's tighter, more cryptic form is more likely original. Bauckham sees 2 Peter as a deliberately constructed *testament* — a pseudonymous letter written in Peter's name as an authoritative elaboration of Jude's warnings for a later generation. On this view, chapter 2 is not a free composition but a careful reworking of Jude, with the Balaam material and the expanded judgment-triad [vv.4–8] representing 2 Peter's distinctive contributions. Commentators who favor Petrine priority (Green, Kruger) argue the reverse: Jude may be a condensation of Petrine material, and the literary direction is not self-evident.

**The δόξας Crux [v.10]**

The disputed referent of δόξας — "angelic majesties" (NASB) or "glorious ones" (ESV) — has occupied commentators from Charles Bigg (*A Critical and Exegetical Commentary on the Epistles of St. Peter and St. Jude*, ICC, 1901) through J.B. Mayor (*The Epistle of St. Jude and the Second Epistle of St. Peter*, 1907), Bauckham, and Michael Green (*The Second Epistle General of Peter and the General Epistle of Jude*, TNTC, 1968). The core question: are the δόξας angelic beings being slandered, or governing human authorities being despised?

Bigg favored a reference to angelic beings, reading the passage in light of the enochic tradition of fallen watchers. Mayor similarly read δόξαι as supernatural beings of high rank. Bauckham argues that the δόξαι are almost certainly angelic beings — not necessarily fallen ones — and that the false teachers, in their pneumatic arrogance, were making blasphemous claims about angelic powers, possibly in a proto-Gnostic context. Green, while cautious, agrees that the most natural parallel is with the angelic beings of the enochic tradition and Jude 8–9. The NASB's "angelic majesties" reflects this consensus; the ESV's "glorious ones" preserves the ambiguity. None of the major commentators reads the referent as straightforwardly human authorities, though the interpretation has been proposed. The crux remains genuinely open, as the translation notes in the phrase-for-phrase layer acknowledge.
