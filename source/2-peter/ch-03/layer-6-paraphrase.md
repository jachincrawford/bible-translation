This is Peter's second letter to the same group of believers, and he has the same goal both times: he wants to wake up the part of their minds that already knows what is true. [vv.1-2] He is not introducing new information. He is reminding them of what the ancient prophets said and what Jesus himself commanded through the apostles who first brought them the message. The point is that they already have what they need — they just need to hold onto it.

The reason this reminder is so urgent is that people are going to show up and mock the whole thing. [vv.3-7] These scoffers will follow their own desires and ask a simple, seemingly reasonable question: "If Jesus promised to come back, where is he? Nothing has changed since the world began." But Peter says they are being deliberately ignorant. They are choosing to forget something crucial from history. God's spoken word is what created the heavens in the first place and formed the earth out of water. That same water later became the instrument of judgment when God flooded and destroyed the ancient world. And right now, that same divine word is holding the present heavens and earth in place — but this time they are being reserved for fire, stored up for the day when God will judge and destroy those who reject him. The pattern is clear: God made the world by his word, he judged it once by water, and he will judge it again — this time by fire.

So why the wait? Peter addresses that head-on. [vv.8-10] He tells his readers not to miss one critical fact: God does not experience time the way we do. To the Lord, a single day is no different from a thousand years, and a thousand years pass like a single day — an idea that goes all the way back to Psalm 90. What looks like slowness from a human point of view is actually patience. God is holding back because he does not want anyone to be destroyed; he wants every person to have the chance to turn their life around. But make no mistake: the day of the Lord will come, and it will come without warning, like a thief arriving when no one expects it. When it happens, the heavens will vanish with a tremendous roar, the basic elements of the universe will be consumed by fire, and the earth and everything people have done on it will be completely exposed — laid bare before God with nothing hidden.

Given all of that, Peter asks the obvious question: what kind of people should you be? [vv.11-13] If the entire physical universe is heading toward that kind of end, the only reasonable response is to live in a way that reflects it — lives marked by holiness and devotion to God. Believers should not dread that day but actually look forward to it and live in a way that moves toward it. Yes, the heavens will be set ablaze and the elements will melt in the heat. But on the other side of that destruction, God has promised something breathtaking: an entirely new heavens and a new earth — a world where everything that is right and good and just finally feels at home, permanently.

Peter closes the letter with practical instructions and a remarkable reference to the apostle Paul. [vv.14-18] Since you are waiting for that new world, he says, do everything you can to be found living at peace with God — clean and without fault. And understand that every day God waits is another day of offered rescue. Paul said the same thing in his letters, writing with the wisdom God gave him. Peter acknowledges that some of what Paul wrote is difficult to understand, and that there are people without knowledge or stability who twist Paul's words — just as they twist the rest of Scripture — and it leads to their own ruin. So be on your guard. You have been warned ahead of time. Do not let the errors of people who reject God's boundaries pull you off the solid ground you are standing on. Instead, keep growing — growing in the grace and the personal knowledge of Jesus Christ, who is both Lord and Savior. The letter ends not with a warning but with worship: to Jesus be the glory, right now and all the way into the day that never ends. Amen.
